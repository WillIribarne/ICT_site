Lesson 1: Interpreting Climate Data & Argument Listening

(40-min lesson plan)

Plan overview:

Students will learn key environment vocabulary, practice reading real climate data and develop both analytical and listening skills. We’ll begin with an interpretive graph task to introduce terms like anomaly, carbon dioxide concentration and trend, then model academic listening by watching a short animated lesson on climate solutions.

Requirements:

  • One computer with Internet, data projector, speakers
  • Websites:
    NASA Vital Signs (https://climate.nasa.gov/vital-signs/)
    TED-Ed's Climate change: Earth’s giant game of Tetris (https://ed.ted.com/lessons/climate-change-earth-s-giant-game-of-tetris-joss-fong)
  • Task sheet (printed copies).

Lesson start!

Task Description
(5-min) Warmer: Project the NASA CO₂ concentration graph. Ask in pairs: “What is the current CO₂ level? How has it changed over 50 years?” Students write down key figures on the print-out and match them to vocabulary definitions you display (e.g., anomaly, parts per million).
(10-min) Web (1): Data hunt (10 min): On the NASA page, students use the “CO₂” slider to identify the decade with the steepest rise. They complete Section A of the NASA Data Worksheet (see below), answering: What was the CO₂ level in 1980 and in 2020? By how many ppm did it increase?
(15-min) Web (2): Argument listening (10 min): Watch the TED-Ed “Tetris” video. Students take notes on the three stages of the carbon cycle as presented. They then answer Section B of the worksheet.
(10-min) What next: In small groups (2 to 3), learners share one surprising data point and one solution they noted. As they speak, you write emerging vocabulary on the board. Finally, each student writes a one-sentence prediction using the target lexis, for example, “If we reduce fossil-fuel use by 50 percent, sea-level rise may slow.”

Lesson 2: Data Exploration & Personal Action Planning

(40-min lesson plan)

Plan overview:

During this 40-minute lesson, students will explore global emissions vs. wealth data and assess their own ecological footprint. They`ll practice filtering interactive charts, drawing conclusions in English, and reflecting on personal behavior.

Requirements:

  • Technology: computer lab or small-group computers; Internet
  • Websites:
    Gapminder Tools: (https://www.gapminder.org/tools/)
    Personal Footprint Calculator: (https://www.footprintcalculator.org/)
  • Task sheet (printed copies).

Lesson start!

Task Description
(5-min) Warmer: Display two quiz questions on the board (e.g., “Which country’s CO₂ per capita is around 5 tons?”). Students guess orally. Reveal answers using Gapminder’s country filter.
(12-min) Web (1): Chart exploration: In pairs, students open Gapminder and set the X-axis to GDP per capita and Y-axis to CO₂ per capita for the latest year. Using the Gapminder Analysis Sheet, they note: Three countries with high GDP but low emissions. Two countries with both high GDP and high emissions.
(13-min) Web (2): Footprint calculation: Individually, learners complete the online footprint survey. They then transfer their personal CO₂ tonnage into the Footprint Reflection Sheet, answering: What was your total footprint? How does it compare to the world average? List two lifestyle changes suggested and rate how feasible each is for you (1 to 5).
(10-min) What next: Students form new groups of four and share one statistic from Gapminder and one action they’ll adopt. Each group writes a concise “Group Commitment” statement (two sentences) using future forms (e.g., “We will cycle to school twice weekly”).
