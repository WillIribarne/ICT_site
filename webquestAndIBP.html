What is the Webquest Model?

The Webquest Model is a form of internet-based project work firstly defined by Bernie Dodge (1995) as "an inquiry-oriented activity in which some or all of the information that learners interact with comes from resources on the Internet."

The structure of the Webquest Model goes as follows:

  1. Introduction: This section sets the scene and introduces the topic, often using visuals, questions, or quotes to get students interested.
  2. Task: This section clearly states the final product students will create. This doesn't have to be digital; it could be a presentation, a role-play, or a poster.
  3. Process: This is the core of the Webquest. The teacher provides step-by-step instructions and specific links to websites. Students complete assigned activities, like answering questions or taking notes, as they explore the links.
  4. Evaluation: This section includes clear assessment criteria for the final product and may also include self-evaluation questions. It's where the teacher gives feedback.
  5. Conclusion: Summarizes the learning experience and offers reflection opportunities.

Are there benefits in doing internet-based projects?

According to Dudeney and Hockly (2008), there are several benefits to internet-based projects:

  • No technical skills nor knowledge are required to start with internet-based projects. Although it takes some time and preparation, they are a structured way for teachers to begin to incorporate internet in the classroom.
  • "They are group activities, and as such, lend themselves to communication and the sharing of knowledge - two principal goals of language teaching itself." (Dudeney & Hockly, 2008, p. 44)
  • Projects usually tackle interdisciplinary fields, making the learning much more enriching and more authentic.
